# Audio Deepfake Detection: Two-Stage Model Design and Deployment  

**Current approaches:** Modern audio deepfake (anti-spoofing) systems use deep neural networks to distinguish real human speech from synthesized speech.  Early methods used hand-crafted spectral features (e.g. MFCC, CQCC) with GMM/SVM classifiers, but recent state-of-the-art (SOTA) models are end-to-end deep nets.  For example, *Light-CNNs (LCNN)* halve model size by replacing ReLU with Max-Feature-Map activation, and have proven effective on benchmarks.  More powerful models use large networks: ResNet-based or graph-based architectures.  Notably, **AASIST** (a graph-attention network on raw audio) set new SOTA, and even its tiny variant **AASIST-L** (only ~85K parameters) “outperforms all competing systems”.  Self-supervised (SSL) speech models are also widely used: pre-trained encoders like Wav2Vec2 or WavLM, fine-tuned for fake-vs-real, give top results.  For instance, Wang *et al.* report that a Wav2Vec2 front-end (fine-tuned with countermeasure layers) achieved the lowest equal-error rates on ASVspoof benchmarks.  Combei *et al.* (2024) similarly found that Wav2Vec2/WavLM features “perform best” and that fine-tuning WavLM gave the best results in the ASVspoof 5 challenge.  In practice, systems often fuse multiple models or features (e.g. combining CNNs, graph nets, SSL encoders) to maximize accuracy.  

**Light vs. heavy models:** There is a range of model sizes.  On the “light” side, small networks have been proposed to reduce cost.  For example, the *ARawNet* architecture uses a lightweight raw-waveform encoder alongside conventional features.  Similarly, Nes2Net processes high-dimensional SSL features with a compact Res2Net back-end, yielding a 22% accuracy gain while cutting back-end computation by 87%.  Even extreme “Green AI” models exist: Wang *et al.* (2024) report ~0.90% EER on ASVspoof 2019 using **<1K parameters**, and note that both training and inference can run efficiently on a **single CPU**.  These tiny models tend to rely on clever feature engineering or frozen SSL embeddings.  In contrast, heavy models include large ResNets or transformers: e.g. fine-tuned Wav2Vec2 “Large” (millions of params) or transformer-based graph nets (AASIST has millions).  The tradeoff is clear: large models give the best generalization and robustness, but small models (or hybrid classical classifiers) drastically cut inference cost.  For example, surveys note that replacing classical features with pre-trained Wav2Vec embeddings “significantly boosts” performance even when using the same back-end, and that SSL front-ends “outperform hand-crafted features” in spoofing tasks.  

**Two-stage cascaded pipeline:** To minimize runtime cost without losing accuracy, a two-stage cascade is advisable.  In this design, **Stage 1** is a very fast, low-complexity detector tuned for high recall (few or no false negatives), and **Stage 2** is a stronger model applied only to suspicious audio segments.  In practice, Stage 1 could be a tiny neural net (e.g. an LCNN or ARawNet-like encoder) or even a classical classifier on extracted features.  It flags any segment that might be fake.  Then Stage 2 (e.g. a fine-tuned Wav2Vec2+graph or transformer model) re-examines only the flagged segments to eliminate false positives.  

- **Stage 1 (Light filter):** Choose an architecture with very few parameters.  Examples include LCNN (using MFM) or an even smaller network distilled from a large model.  It may use simpler features (e.g. MFCCs or frozen SSL embeddings).  A popular strategy is to train this filter with a loose threshold to ensure almost all true deepfakes are caught (even if it triggers on some genuine audio).  For instance, Pindrop researchers describe training a binary spoof detector first, then freezing it and adding a lightweight classifier on its embeddings; this two-stage approach is “attractive in practice due to reduced computational costs”.  Similarly, knowledge-distillation techniques can compress a large model into a small one.  In any case, Stage 1 must be extremely fast: e.g. Wang *et al.* achieved near-SOTA accuracy on ASVspoof with a model of only ~1K parameters that can run in real-time on CPU.  

- **Stage 2 (Heavy verifier):** Apply a high-capacity model only to segments flagged by Stage 1.  This model can be a SOTA deep net (e.g. AASIST or a fine-tuned Wav2Vec2/WavLM).  Since Stage 1 has already filtered out most obviously-real speech, Stage 2 runs rarely (e.g. <20% of the time).  Its job is to reduce false positives and ensure final precision.  By design, Stage 2 can be slower/higher-accuracy because it processes much less audio.  For example, if Stage 2 uses a 94M-parameter Wav2Vec2, it might take 50–100 ms per second of audio on a GPU, but if invoked only for 10% of segments, the *average* cost is reduced by ~90%.  (Exact savings depend on the filtering rate and hardware.)  In other domains, similar cascaded classifiers (fast filter + slow checker) are common to balance speed and accuracy.  

- **Cost estimation:**  Roughly, total inference cost ≈ Cost(Stage1) + Cost(Stage2)×(fraction flagged).  If Stage1 is very cheap (say 10× faster than Stage2) and flags only a small fraction, total cost can approach that of Stage1 alone.  For instance, if Stage1 flags 20% of audio, overall FLOPs ≈0.2×Stage2 + 0.8×Stage1.  Many practical setups could run Stage1 on-device (CPU) and Stage2 on GPU or a server.  Indeed, Green-AI work shows small counters can detect spoofing at <1% error on a CPU.  Meanwhile, heavy deep models (e.g. fine-tuned Wav2Vec2 or AASIST) remain on standby for flagged clips.  

**Training vs. fine-tuning:** In choosing models, fine-tuning pre-trained networks is usually advantageous.  Pre-trained speech encoders (Wav2Vec2, WavLM, HuBERT, Whisper) have learned general acoustic patterns that improve robustness.  Studies consistently show fine-tuned SSL models outperform scratch-trained ones.  For example, Tak *et al.* achieved *“the lowest equal error rates reported”* by using a Wav2Vec2 front-end fine-tuned for spoof detection.  Combei *et al.* similarly found Wav2Vec2/WavLM to be best among ten evaluated front-ends.  Fine-tuning also yields much faster convergence and generalizes better to unseen attacks.  The drawback is computational cost: training a 100M+ model requires GPUs and many samples.  However, transfer learning allows using smaller labeled datasets effectively.  

In contrast, training a model from scratch (especially a small one) avoids reliance on pre-trained weights and can be more customizable.  But scratch-training usually needs careful augmentation or one-class strategies to avoid overfitting.  A hybrid approach is to freeze a foundation model’s layers and train only a light classifier on top (as in [51]).  Knowledge distillation is another hybrid: one can train a large teacher and distill its knowledge into a small student for Stage 1.  In practice, it is often best to leverage existing checkpoints.  Hugging Face and literature provide many fine-tuned deepfake detectors (e.g. Wav2Vec2 checkpoints).  For Stage 2, fine-tuning a proven model is recommended.  For Stage 1, one could either train a small model from scratch or distill a large model; given the goal (fast inference), using pre-extracted features (e.g. fixed Wav2Vec embeddings) with a tiny trainable head can be very efficient.

**Recommendation:**  We suggest a cascaded design: a *fast binary filter* followed by a *high-capacity verifier*.  For example:  

- **Stage 1:** A lightweight CNN or TDNN on spectral inputs (or a distilled shallow network on SSL features).  Architectures like LCNN or ARawNet are good templates.  Train it with a low threshold to catch almost all fakes (maximizing recall, allowing more false alarms).  It can be heavily quantized or pruned to run on CPU.  

- **Stage 2:** A SOTA anti-spoof model.  Candidates include AASIST or an SSL backbone (e.g. Wav2Vec2 or WavLM) with a classifier.  *Nes2Net* (Res2Net on Wav2Vec features) is another strong option.  This model is only run on segments Stage 1 flags.  It should be fine-tuned on your targeted deepfake data.  

- **Deployment:** Split incoming audio into short overlapping windows (e.g. 3–5 s).  Stage 1 evaluates each window; if flagged, pass that window (or sub-window) to Stage 2.  The system then outputs decisions per segment.  This reduces load: Stage 1 handles all audio cheaply, Stage 2 only the suspects.  Keep monitoring Stage 1’s false-negative rate (missed fakes), adjusting its threshold to avoid leaking fakes.  In practice, two-stage schemes are already used in related tasks: e.g. Pindrop’s two-stage model “leverages existing binary systems” to cut cost.  

- **Benchmarks:**  On ASVspoof tasks, heavy models routinely reach EER <1%, while tiny models (like the Green AI example) reached ~0.9% EER.  In a two-stage system, you’d aim for Stage 1 to err on the side of over-flagging (so overall recall ≈100%), then rely on Stage 2 to refine precision.  For latency, expect Stage 1 inference on the order of a few milliseconds per second of audio (or less, on GPU/CPU), and Stage 2 tens of milliseconds when invoked.  Overall, if Stage 2 is triggered, end-to-end detection for that segment might take ~50–100 ms; if not triggered, Stage 1 alone might take ~5–10 ms.  

**Summary:**  A cascaded detector – a very fast, small-parameter model followed by a heavyweight verifier – can greatly reduce average inference cost while preserving detection quality.  Use a high-recall Stage 1 (e.g. LCNN or small TDNN) and a SOTA Stage 2 (e.g. fine-tuned Wav2Vec2/AASIST).  Favor transfer learning for the heavyweight stage, as it yields superior robustness.  With appropriate thresholds and a well-tuned Stage 1, this two-stage flow minimizes wasted compute on genuine speech.  Knowledge distillation or classical ML on SSL embeddings may also help design the lightweight filter.  In deployment, this architecture should be benchmarked on target hardware to confirm the latency/cost gains.
